# Voice Profile: GSV Portfolio (Project Pages)

Context: Public-facing project pages for the General Systems Ventures portfolio at generalsystemsventures.com. These are short-form descriptions (1-4 sentences of body copy plus frontmatter metadata) that present projects to technically literate visitors. The voice blends MK's base nonfiction profile with an archaeological, taste-driven sensibility distinct from both the blog voice (institutional) and the docs voice (internal).

## Overview

Where Apple says "here's what's next," GSV says "here's what was lost, here's what survived, and here's what one's taste demanded be rebuilt." Projects are justified by lineage and personal history, not by market gaps or feature lists. The voice is that of someone who builds things because their taste demands they exist.

The register sits between the base profile's conversational Substack energy and the blog voice's institutional composure. It is formal without being corporate, personal without being confessional. The core influences are:

- **Gibson's Blue Ant trilogy** — archaeological tech aesthetic, brand-as-taste, compressed evocative descriptions of dead technology
- **Paul Ford** — sardonic formality, technical explained via cultural, parenthetical second voice
- **Craig Mod** — craft specificity, personal history as product motivation, sensory memory as origin story
- **Stewart Brand** — shearing layers, what persists vs what gets replaced, Long Now sensibility
- **Venkatesh Rao** — compressed aphorisms, double meanings in minimum words, systems thinking as style
- **Panic Inc. (Cabel Sasser)** — warm nostalgia for dead software, products justified by taste, playful specificity
- **Robin Sloan** — register-blending (formal to fairy-tale to technical within a paragraph), warmth alongside precision
- **Teenage Engineering** — design-object-as-personality, playful minimalism, product names and copy that feel like they were made by someone who actually enjoys making things

## Sentence Structure

Inherit the base profile's medium-length declaratives and colon/semicolon pivots, but vary structure across pages. No two project pages should share the same skeleton. Possible shapes:

- Personal origin story → what it does → design principle (Enozo)
- Problem stated bluntly → solution (AgMoDB)
- Contrast via semicolon pivot → mechanism (DueLLM)
- Comparison → what agents do → tagline + loss condition (Elf Revel)
- Claim → contrast → empirical proof (Sylveste)

Each page earns its own shape based on what makes the project interesting. If a draft reads like a template applied to different content, the structure is wrong.

## Vocabulary & Diction

**"One's" for impersonal address.** Not "your" (too direct for portfolio), not passive (too stiff). "Whether one's orchestration approach actually works" is the register.

**Name specific components.** "Sylveste's Intercore kernel" not "the kernel." "Claude Haiku" not "an AI advisor." Proper nouns over generic references when the component has a name.

**Stronger verbs.** "Empowers the routing layer" over "lets the routing layer." "Replaces that folly" over "replaces that." Verbs should be specific and active, not casual or vague.

**Sardonic formality.** "An exercise in vibes," "that folly," "now and forever." The register of someone who finds the state of things genuinely funny without performing the joke.

**Implementation language is irrelevant.** Don't mention Rust, Python, TypeScript unless the language choice is load-bearing to the project's identity. Portfolio visitors care about what the thing does, not what it's written in.

## Tone & Voice

**Archaeological, not aspirational.** Projects are framed by what came before them: dead software that inspired them (iZotope Ozone for Winamp), design traditions they continue or reject (Dwarf Fortress), cultural references adapted to the domain (Castlevania's "miserable little pile of tokens"). The past is not nostalgia; it is the reason the present project exists.

**Cultural eulogy as motivation.** "The plugin ecosystem for something like Winamp died just as things like Winamp did" — willing to name what was lost and frame the project as what survived or was rebuilt.

**Self-deprecating warmth.** The Castlevania reference, "when the vibes are off," "(more likely laptop)" — humor through adapted cultural references and parenthetical asides. Never forced; always earned by surrounding density.

**Personal origin stories.** When a project has a biographical origin (Enozo/iZotope), lead with it. The specificity of personal history is the strongest signal that a real person built this, not a company filling a product grid.

**Not selling — showing.** These pages explain why something exists and what it does. They do not pitch, persuade, or position. No calls to action, no competitive framing, no market language.

## Structure Patterns

**Frontmatter fields are copy too.** Taglines, lineages, and descriptions need the same voice treatment as body copy. They are not metadata to be filled generically.

**Lineages use colons, not em dashes.** "Aesthetic entropy: a colony sim where the loss condition is creative stagnation" — the colon sets up an explanation. Em dashes are banned in lineage fields.

**Taglines should be pithy and distinctive.** "Reveling elves communally crafting critical hits" over "Elven colony simulator driven by aesthetic conflict." Wordplay and double meanings are welcome. Platform/API names as tagline identity (e.g., "macOS Core Audio enhancement") read as Apple brand voice, not GSV.

**Descriptions can match body copy** when the body is strong enough to serve as both page content and meta preview. No need to write two versions of the same text.

**Compressed wordplay in lineages.** "An agency peripheral for peripheral agency" — double meanings compressed to minimum words. These are the Gibson-coded moments.

## Cultural References

More than the blog voice, less than the base profile's essay register. References should be:

- Adapted to the domain ("miserable little pile of tokens" from Castlevania)
- Hyper-specific enough that recognition is rewarding ("iZotope Ozone for Winamp")
- Non-load-bearing (the sentence works for readers who miss the reference)
- From personal history when possible (things MK actually used, played, read)

The reference set includes: science fiction (Culture novels, Revelation Space, The Peripheral, Castlevania), dead software (Winamp, plugin ecosystems), game design (Dwarf Fortress, Diablo), and the same media theory / political anthropology / internet culture as the base profile.

## Anti-Patterns

All base profile anti-patterns carry over, plus:

**No uniform structure across pages.** If every page follows the same sentence-count or rhetorical pattern, the portfolio reads as templated. Each page needs its own shape.

**No stacked fragment closers.** "Open source. Named after Revelation Space." — short sentences stacked at paragraph end are an AI writing tic, not an MK pattern.

**No tack-on em dashes.** Em dashes used to bolt extra information onto the end of an already-complete sentence ("which works well — and the whole thing is open source") are formulaic. If closing facts need to land, make them structurally load-bearing.

**No platform/API as identity.** "macOS Core Audio enhancement" sounds like Apple developer documentation. The project's identity comes from what it does or why it exists, not which API it calls.

**No "not X, but Y" as default framing.** This construction is fine when genuinely correcting a misconception. It is not fine as the default way to introduce what makes a project interesting.

**Colons over em dashes in metadata.** Lineage and description fields use colons for the pivot, not em dashes.

**Two-item lists over three.** Reinforce the base profile's rule-of-three ban. Prefer pairs or four-item lists. Three is acceptable only when the content is genuinely tripartite (e.g., three tiers of intentionality).
