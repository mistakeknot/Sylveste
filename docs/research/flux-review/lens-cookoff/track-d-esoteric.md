## Yoruba Ifa Odu Verse Selection

### Source Domain: Yoruba religious epistemology, Oyo/Osogbo lineages, pre-colonial to present

### Core Mechanism: Recall-order bias in selection from a memorized corpus — senior babalawos compensate for primacy fixation (early-memorized verses crowding out later-learned ones) through rotational recitation that deliberately varies the starting position of each practice cycle. Cross-lineage agreement (Oyo vs. Ekiti) counts as stronger evidence of genuine fit than within-lineage agreement, because same-lineage babalawos share memorization order and therefore share recall bias.

### Findings

- **[P0] Unweighted agreement conflates genuine convergence with correlated training bias**: `analyze_cookoff.py` lines 87–112 compute agreement by counting how many models voted for each lens, then marking consensus at ≥60% (`v / n >= 0.6`). `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 77–113 refine this using only `LOW_BIAS_MODELS = {"gpt-5.4-mini", "llama-4-maverick", "command-r-plus", "deepseek-v3.2"}` — but the filter is applied to exclude high-bias models, not to weight by model-family independence. A 4-0 agreement among low-bias models where 3 of the 4 are GPT variants (or 3 are OpenRouter-hosted instruction-tuned Chinese models sharing RLHF pipeline details) is treated identically to a 4-0 agreement spanning Anthropic + OpenAI + DeepSeek + Nvidia architectures. The Ifa parallel: three Oyo-lineage babalawos agreeing means less than one Oyo and one Ekiti agreeing, because Oyo lineages share a memorization order. The `LOW_BIAS_MODELS` set as currently defined contains `gpt-5.4-mini` and `command-r-plus` (same OpenAI API family, potentially correlated RLHF training) alongside `deepseek-v3.2` (independent Chinese lab). Agreement between `gpt-5.4-mini` and `command-r-plus` should carry less weight than agreement between either of them and `deepseek-v3.2`. The fix is small: add a `MODEL_FAMILIES` mapping in `build_anchor_suite.py` and require that `corrected_consensus` lenses pass both the ≥50% threshold AND appear in the agreement set of at least two distinct families — one line of filtering after line 93.

- **[P1] Anchor suite admits high-agreement pairings where position bias is the cause of agreement**: `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 143–145 filter anchors by `corrected_agreement >= min_agreement` AND `corrected_consensus` being non-empty — but `corrected_agreement` is computed from `LOW_BIAS_MODELS`, and that set was defined in an earlier investigation (`README.md` line 85: `gpt-5.4-nano 12.8%, command-r-plus 22%`). A lens at index 1 (SBI, the documented "SBI attractor" at ~38% selection rate) can still appear in `corrected_consensus` for a dilemma — the position-bias filter demoted high-bias models but did not require that the agreed-upon lens itself has low selection frequency across the corpus. The Ifa equivalent: removing babalawos with strong primacy fixation from the training panel while still accepting the most-commonly-retrieved verse as canonical. The anchor suite will systematically over-represent early-index lenses as "expected_lenses," and every future regression run will reinforce this. Fix: in `select_anchors`, cross-reference `corrected_consensus` lenses against a frequency baseline; any lens in the top-10 by corpus-wide selection rate should require a higher agreement threshold (e.g., ≥0.80 corrected vs. ≥0.70) to qualify as an anchor lens.

- **[P2] Zero-selection responses are filtered as missing data before agreement computation**: `analyze_cookoff.py` line 56 filters `ok = [r for r in results if not r["error"]]` — this correctly preserves zero-lens selections (they are not errors). However, `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 68–73 count `empty_count` but then lines 100–103 compute `agreement` by dividing `consensus_votes_sum / total_votes` — when `total_votes == 0` (all models selected zero lenses for a dilemma), the fallback is `1.0 if empty_count == n else 0.0`. This correctly treats universal silence as perfect agreement. But lines 143–145 require `corrected_consensus` to be non-empty for a dilemma to enter the anchor set, which means dilemmas where all low-bias models silently agree on "no lens applies" are excluded from the anchor suite entirely. The Ifa silence-as-signal principle: *aito ese* (no applicable verse) is diagnostically valuable — it indicates the querent's question may be malformed, or a gap in the corpus. These "universal silence" dilemmas should constitute a distinct anchor type (e.g., `"type": "null_anchor"`) with `"expected_lenses": []` explicitly, so regression tests can verify the system does not force a lens onto a dilemma the calibration panel unanimously left blank.

- **[P3] Near-miss data captures only the final selection, not the probability mass over the full 291-lens space**: The cookoff records `selected_lenses` (a list of 0-3 names) and `selected_indices` (the integer array). For a dilemma where 7 models selected lens A and 5 selected lens B, the per-model records preserve this — but there is no record of whether each model "nearly selected" lens C with high internal probability. The Ifa tradition's most diagnostically valuable moment is the babalawo's hesitation between two verses; the near-miss signal in this pipeline is reconstructed from *cross-model disagreement* as a proxy for *within-model uncertainty*. This is a legitimate proxy but it misses cases where all 12 models agreed on lens A but 11 of them also nearly selected lens B — a structural ambiguity in the lens library that cross-model agreement statistics cannot detect. Does the prompt format (returning a bare JSON array) make it impossible to capture this signal? If so, a parallel run with confidence-scored output (`[{"lens": 3, "confidence": 0.9}, {"lens": 7, "confidence": 0.6}]`) on a 10% sample would reveal this hidden near-miss topology without replacing the current format.

## Byzantine Iconographic Program Selection

### Source Domain: Middle Byzantine church decoration, 9th–12th century, Cappadocia to Constantinople

### Core Mechanism: Hierarchical constraint distortion — the apse/dome/pendentive positions are theologically mandated (Pantokrator in the dome, Theotokos Platytera in the apse), producing near-universal agreement across all independent iconographers. This forced agreement at the top of the hierarchy drowns out the meaningful divergence at mid-rank nave positions, where genuine selection judgment enters. A cross-church agreement analysis that does not stratify by position rank systematically overweights the uninformative constrained positions.

### Findings

- **[P0] All 12 models receive the 291-lens library in identical presentation order with no shuffled-order control**: `lens_cookoff.py` lines 58–72 build a single `lens_index` string from the lens library in its natural order. Line 307 calls `build_lens_index()` once, and line 357 passes the same `lens_index` to every `call_model` invocation across all 12 models and all 1,360 dilemmas. The 291 lenses are presented in the same sequence to every model in every run. This means all 12 models share the same attention-decay curve — early-position lenses receive stronger attention from all of them simultaneously. The Byzantine parallel: the apse constraint forces all iconographers to place the same subject there; the fixed lens order forces all models to process the same lenses with peak attention. Agreement statistics computed from this setup cannot distinguish "this lens genuinely fits this dilemma" from "this lens appears at position 1-10 and all models have higher selection probability for early-seen items." The `analyze_cookoff.py` position bias section (lines 209–237) measures which models over-select indices ≤10, but since all models see the same order, this measures model-specific bias (how much more than baseline does model X select early lenses) — not the shared baseline elevation caused by the fixed ordering itself. Fix: in `build_lens_index()`, accept an optional `seed` parameter and shuffle the lenses before building the string. Run at least one model family (e.g., the 4 OpenRouter paid models) with `seed=42` and another with `seed=99`, then compare agreement scores across orderings. One additional parameter and one additional `random.shuffle` call.

- **[P1] The anchor suite is constructed from a single evaluation pass with no bootstrap validation**: `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 133–225 construct the anchor suite in a single pass over `cookoff_results.jsonl`. There is no holdout set, no cross-validation fold, and no mechanism to detect whether the first-round evaluations themselves were systematically biased before their consensus is enshrined as `"expected_lenses"`. The Byzantine parallel: the Hermeneia began as Patriarch Photios's contextual 9th-century preferences and became an 18th-century canonical rule — once written down, the codification became self-reinforcing. If the initial cookoff run was conducted with a particular lens ordering (it was — single fixed order), the anchor suite encodes whatever position bias existed in that run as ground truth. All future regression runs will then measure drift from a biased baseline and interpret it as degradation, while the original bias is invisible. Fix: split the 12 models into two independent panels (e.g., models 1-6 and models 7-12) before building the anchor suite. Compute `corrected_agreement` within each panel independently, and only promote dilemma-lens pairings where both panels agree. This halves panel size but provides cross-validation that the current design entirely lacks.

- **[P2] Agreement analysis weights all agreement levels equally, discarding the mid-agreement calibration signal**: `analyze_cookoff.py` lines 96–113 compute a single `score` per dilemma (fraction of votes in the consensus bucket) and produce two outputs: top-15 disagreement and top-15 agreement. `build_anchor_suite.py` applies a threshold at `min_agreement = 0.7` and rejects everything below it. The mid-agreement band — dilemmas where 5-9 of 12 models agree, representing genuine competing-lens cases — is classified as "near-miss" and used for disambiguation testing, not calibration. The Byzantine finding is that nave positions (mid-rank, where the hierarchy is loose but not absent) contain the highest-density calibration signal: neither mandated nor random, but genuinely judged. The pipeline's near-miss set (low agreement) is valuable but distinct from the mid-agreement band (partial consensus). A dilemma where 8/12 models select lens A and 4 select lens B is not a "near-miss" in the same sense as a dilemma where 3 models each select 4 different lenses. The near-miss extraction in `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 169–175 uses `agreement < 0.3 and empty_count < n * 0.5` OR `len(contested_lenses) >= 2` — this does capture some mid-agreement cases via the contested-lens criterion, but it does not explicitly privilege the 5-9/12 band as the highest-information tier for understanding the lens library's structure.

- **[P2] Provider-family lens signatures are not captured as diagnostic output**: `analyze_cookoff.py` computes per-model stats (lines 192–207) and per-model position bias (lines 209–237) but does not group models by provider family and report which lens clusters each family systematically favors or avoids. The Byzantine parallel: Cappadocian churches systematically include local monastic saints absent from Constantinopolitan programs — this regional signature reveals training-lineage differences, not quality differences. If OpenRouter-hosted Chinese models (Qwen, GLM, DeepSeek) systematically favor lenses rooted in systems-thinking or game-theoretic frameworks, while Anthropic/OpenAI models favor interpersonal/ethical frameworks, the current pipeline reports this only implicitly in per-model lens frequencies. A provider-family aggregation table — 4 rows (Anthropic, OpenAI, Chinese labs, Nvidia) × top-10 lenses — would make this diagnostic explicit and allow downstream users of the anchor suite to understand which `expected_lenses` reflect genuine consensus vs. provider-family co-bias.

- **[P3] Cross-dilemma lens coherence is not analyzed**: The 1,360 dilemmas span 17 topic groups with 80 dilemmas each. Within a topic group, structurally similar dilemmas (e.g., two "workplace" dilemmas about whistleblowing) might be expected to produce similar lens selections from a well-calibrated model. The pipeline treats each dilemma as fully independent. The feast-cycle constraint in Byzantine iconography teaches that coherent selection across related cases is a signal of principled judgment — an iconographer who includes one scene from the Passion cycle but not the adjacent scenes is making an error, not a creative choice. Does a model that selects lens X for dilemma 47 also select it for structurally similar dilemmas 48-52? A within-topic-group coherence analysis (correlation of lens selections across dilemma pairs in the same topic group) would distinguish models that apply lenses consistently (principled) from those that apply them erratically (noisy), independent of whether any individual selection is correct.

## Akkadian Baru Hepatoscopy Classification

### Source Domain: Old Babylonian and Middle Assyrian divination, Mari, Sippar, Nineveh, c. 1800–600 BCE

### Core Mechanism: Prototype-anchoring bias — the clay liver models used to train apprentice baru priests were simultaneously the foundation of expertise and the source of systematic distortion. Real liver variation is continuous; the training prototypes are discrete. Distinctive clay models create attractor basins that pull ambiguous real livers toward themselves regardless of fit. The baru's classification fidelity depends not only on the priest's judgment but on how evenly the training prototypes were distributed across the feature space — uneven distribution means some regions of the space have strong attractors while others have none.

### Findings

- **[P0] All models process the 291 lenses in identical order, making position bias a shared confound rather than a detectable per-model artifact**: `lens_cookoff.py` line 291 assembles `user_input` from the single global `lens_index` string (built once at line 307). This is identical to the Byzantine P0 finding but from a different causal mechanism: the Babylonian issue is that all baru priests were trained with the same zone-examination order (gate of the palace first, always), making it impossible to detect whether classification errors come from the zone-order training or from the individual priest's judgment. When all 12 models see the same lens ordering, the position-bias statistic in `analyze_cookoff.py` lines 215–228 measures each model's INCREMENTAL bias above the shared baseline — but the shared baseline elevation is invisible, and the random baseline of `3.4%` (10/291, line 231) assumes uniform probability which the fixed ordering violates. The position-bias "grade" of "low/medium/high" in the current code is computed relative to the random baseline, but the actual baseline for early-position lenses is elevated for ALL models by the ordering effect. A model graded "low" at 12% top-10 selection may still have substantial shared ordering bias that the metric cannot see. Fix: add `random.shuffle(lenses)` with a run-specific seed in `build_lens_index()` before constructing the index string, and record which seed was used in each cookoff result record. This makes position bias a between-run observable rather than a within-run invisible.

- **[P1] Lens description properties (length, concreteness, named examples) are not analyzed as covariates of selection frequency**: `analyze_cookoff.py` lines 48–72 compute `lens_freq` — a raw count of how often each lens was selected. The top-30 and bottom-10 outputs report frequency but provide no explanation of WHY some lenses are selected frequently. The Babylonian diagnostic: does over-selection correlate with clay model distinctiveness (unmistakable features) rather than omen applicability? In the Auraken context: does lens selection frequency correlate with description length, the presence of a named example (e.g., "SBI: Situation-Behavior-Impact" anchors on a memorable acronym), or description concreteness? The SBI attractor at ~38% selection rate (`README.md` line 94) is exactly what the Babylonian model predicts — a lens with a crisp, named-example description creates an attractor basin that pulls ambiguous dilemmas toward it. The pipeline currently has no way to test whether SBI is selected 38% of the time because it genuinely applies 38% of the time OR because its description is more vivid than the 290 other lenses. Fix: join the per-lens selection frequencies from `lens_freq` against lens metadata from `lens_library_v2.json` (description length, whether the name is an acronym, number of sentences). Report this correlation in `analyze_cookoff.py`. This is a 10-line addition after line 72 that would immediately reveal whether the frequency distribution tracks description properties.

- **[P2] Near-miss records do not include competing-lens identity gaps or explicit boundary annotations**: `build_anchor_suite.py` lines 198–212 build near-miss records with `contested_lenses` (a list) and `all_votes` (a dict of lens → vote count). This preserves the full vote distribution, which is good. What is missing is the BOUNDARY annotation: for each contested pair (lens A vs. lens B), is this boundary consistent across many dilemmas (revealing a structural ambiguity in the lens library) or idiosyncratic (one dilemma where A and B happen to both apply)? The baruti "LIKE but not" notation was powerful because it was systematic — the compendia tracked boundary cases across hundreds of liver observations, mapping where prototype A ended and prototype B began. The current pipeline produces per-dilemma near-miss records but has no aggregation step that asks: "across all 50 near-miss dilemmas, which lens pairs appear together as contested options most frequently?" A lens-pair co-contestation matrix (which pairs of lenses are simultaneously contested across many dilemmas) would map the lens library's boundary topology — the equivalent of the baruti mapping the anatomical boundary between the "weapon" zone and the "finger" zone. This is a post-processing step on the `near_misses` list in the output of `build_anchor_suite.py`, not a change to the core pipeline.

- **[P2] The training set (lens descriptions) is treated as fixed ground truth; no mechanism evaluates the lens descriptions themselves for anchoring asymmetry**: `build_anchor_suite.py` constructs anchor and near-miss suites to calibrate future model behavior against the current lens library. But if the lens library itself has uneven description vividness — some lenses with rich, example-laden descriptions and others with terse abstract descriptions — the calibration process will treat the vividness gap as a fact of nature rather than a correctable property. The Babylonian lesson is the deepest one: the clay liver models were the most important training artifact, and their design determined the quality of all subsequent classification. The pipeline's calibration infrastructure is sophisticated, but there is no corresponding audit of `lens_library_v2.json` for description evenness. Does the current pipeline have any mechanism (or planned mechanism) to feed the near-miss and frequency data BACK into improving lens descriptions — adding contrast annotations for frequently-confused lens pairs, or adding specificity to under-selected lenses with terse descriptions? Without this feedback loop, calibration corrects the classifiers while the training set remains uncorrected, and the same biases will re-emerge in future model runs.

- **[P3] The "dropped models" list in README.md contains exactly the diagnostic information the baruti preserved as minority opinions, and it is discarded**: `README.md` lines 62–79 document the 15 dropped models with reasons. Several of these are highly informative: `llama-4-maverick` selects zero lenses 97.8% of the time (an extreme silence signal), `step-3.5-flash` selects 0 lenses every time, `kimi-k2.5` selects 0 lenses 80% of the time. The baruti compendia explicitly preserved minority classifications and variant readings — not because they were correct, but because their systematic divergence from the majority mapped the edges of the classification system. The zero-selection behavior of these dropped models is not noise; it is a coherent signal that certain model architectures treat the lens-selection task as almost always inapplicable. Does the pipeline preserve the dropped-model results (even partially) in a separate analysis tier, or are they permanently excluded? A single-pass analysis of the dropped-model results — even just the frequency of zero-selection vs. model characteristics — would reveal whether the silence is random (broken models) or systematic (a meaningful judgment that the 291 lenses rarely apply to these dilemmas).
