### Summary

The brainstorm proposes a compelling vision where 10 subsystems in a capability mesh earn trust independently through evidence signals, with overall autonomy determined by the minimum maturity. From a credit rating methodology perspective, this is a multi-source composite score problem — and the brainstorm skips the hardest part: the aggregation methodology. Credit rating agencies publish detailed methodologies explaining how they weight individual factors, how they handle factors measured on different scales, and how they produce a final rating. The v5.0 brainstorm has the individual factors (evidence signals per subsystem) but no published weighting, no scale normalization, and no methodology for deriving the composite. The "minimum of subsystem maturities" rule is one specific (and unusual) aggregation function that has known failure modes.

### Issues Found

CRM-1. P0: **No commensurability mechanism for cross-subsystem evidence.** The capability mesh (Section: Key Decisions, Decision 2) defines evidence signals per subsystem: "gate pass rate" (Routing), "query hit rate, confidence scores" (Ontology), "conflict resolution rate, sync latency" (Integration), "finding precision, false positive rate" (Review). These metrics use different scales (percentages, ratios, milliseconds, counts), different semantics (higher-is-better vs lower-is-better), and different distributions. The brainstorm claims "the system's overall autonomy is the minimum of its subsystem maturities" (line 98) but does not specify how raw metrics are converted to comparable maturity scores. You cannot take the minimum of 73% gate pass rate and 42ms sync latency — they are incommensurable. In credit ratings, this is solved by mapping each factor to a common ordinal scale (e.g., AAA-D) with explicit mapping criteria for each factor. Without this, the "minimum" rule produces arbitrary results depending on how the mapping is implicitly done. Failure scenario: Two operators evaluating the same system state disagree on which subsystem is the bottleneck because they mentally normalize the raw metrics differently. The minimum rule gives deterministic results only when inputs are already on a common scale.

CRM-2. P1: **Goodhart's Law exposure in evidence signals.** The flywheel (Section: Why This Approach, The Flywheel) creates an incentive loop: better evidence signals → more trust → more autonomy → more evidence. This is precisely the dynamic that produces Goodhart's Law failures. The PHILOSOPHY.md already acknowledges this ("Rotate and diversify. No single metric stays dominant. Diverse evaluation resists Goodhart pressure," line 53-54), but the brainstorm's evidence signals are static per subsystem — each cell has a fixed set. There is no signal rotation schedule, no mechanism for detecting when a proxy has decoupled from its outcome, and no independent validation that the proxy still predicts the thing it's supposed to predict. In credit ratings, the 2008 financial crisis demonstrated what happens when ratings agencies optimize for metrics (default rates on structured products) that stop correlating with actual credit risk. Failure scenario: The Routing subsystem's "gate pass rate" starts high because agents learn to game the gates (producing outputs that pass the check but don't actually improve downstream quality), and the evidence pipeline compounds this gaming signal as genuine improvement.

CRM-3. P1: **No published methodology for trust score derivation.** Credit rating agencies are required (post-Dodd-Frank) to publish their rating methodologies — including factor weights, adjustment rules, and override policies — so that rated entities and investors can independently reconstruct the rating. The brainstorm describes evidence signals but never publishes a methodology for how evidence → maturity assessment. Key questions unanswered: (a) What weights do different evidence signals carry within a single subsystem? (b) Can qualitative factors (e.g., "authority ratchet events") override quantitative signals? (c) What is the recency weighting — does evidence from 30 days ago count equally to evidence from yesterday? (d) Is there a "rating committee" process where multiple evaluators jointly assess, or is the maturity score purely algorithmic? Failure scenario: The trust system becomes a black box that operators do not understand, eroding the very trust it claims to build. If a human cannot trace "this subsystem is at maturity level 3 because..." then the evidence-based trust thesis is undermined.

CRM-4. P1: **Point-in-time vs through-the-cycle conflation.** The flywheel assumes evidence compounds over time, but the brainstorm does not distinguish between point-in-time assessment (what is the current state of evidence right now?) and through-the-cycle assessment (what is the trend-adjusted state accounting for cyclical variation?). In credit ratings, this is a foundational distinction: point-in-time ratings are volatile and useful for trading; through-the-cycle ratings are stable and useful for long-term capital allocation. The capability mesh appears to use point-in-time signals (current gate pass rate, current sync latency) but the authority ratchet implies through-the-cycle behavior (trust accumulates and is not instantly revoked). This creates a mismatch: a subsystem could have a bad day (point-in-time degradation) while its through-the-cycle trend is improving, or vice versa. Failure scenario: The authority ratchet prematurely promotes a subsystem based on a short-term spike in evidence quality (point-in-time), ignoring a deteriorating through-the-cycle trend.

CRM-5. P2: **No rating transition matrix.** Credit rating agencies publish transition matrices showing the historical probability of migrating between rating levels (e.g., 5% chance of downgrade from AA to A in any given year). The capability mesh defines maturity levels per subsystem but does not model the expected transition dynamics. How long should a subsystem typically spend at each maturity level? What is the expected demotion rate? What are the absorption states (maturity levels that systems tend to stay in)? Without this, operators have no baseline for evaluating whether the system is progressing normally or stalling. Failure scenario: A subsystem has been at the same maturity level for 6 months, and no one knows whether this is normal (the average dwell time) or concerning (the subsystem is stuck).

### Improvements

IMP-1. Define an explicit ordinal maturity scale (e.g., M0-M4 or similar) with named levels per subsystem. For each subsystem, publish a factor-to-maturity mapping that converts raw evidence signals to the ordinal scale. Example: Routing maturity M0 = gate pass rate < 50%, M1 = 50-70%, M2 = 70-85%, M3 = 85-95%, M4 = >95% with no regression over 30 days. This makes the "minimum" rule operational.

IMP-2. Add a proxy validation mechanism to the flywheel: periodically correlate each evidence signal with the downstream outcome it is supposed to predict, and flag signals where the correlation has dropped below a threshold. This is the anti-Goodhart mechanism that the PHILOSOPHY.md already calls for but the brainstorm doesn't instantiate.

IMP-3. Publish a "Maturity Assessment Methodology" document (analogous to a rating methodology) that specifies: factor identification, factor weighting, scale mapping, recency adjustment, aggregation function, override policy, and assessment frequency. This is the artifact that makes the evidence-based trust claim auditable.

IMP-4. Introduce a distinction between "current maturity assessment" (point-in-time, for operational decisions) and "maturity trajectory" (through-the-cycle, for planning decisions). The authority ratchet should use through-the-cycle assessments for promotion/demotion and point-in-time assessments for operational alerts.
